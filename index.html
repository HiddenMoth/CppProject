What is Writing?
Who is a Writer?

A digital final project exploring the nature of writing and what it means to be a writer by Jose Lopez.

What is Writing?

Writing is understood differently by everyone. As a mathematics major and someone who spends a lot of time hiking, I see writing not only as words on a screen or page, but as patterns, structures, and traces that carry information over time.

In math, writing shows up in the form of symbols, equations, proofs, and graphs. A proof is a kind of narrative: it takes you from assumptions to a conclusion step by step. A function written as f(x) on a page is a compact way of describing an entire relationship. These marks may look abstract, but they communicate precise ideas to anyone who has learned how to read them.

Hiking and geology offer a different kind of writing. Trails, rock layers, and river channels are physical records of processes unfolding over long periods of time. Fluvial erosion, for example—water carving its way through rock and soil—writes a story into the landscape. From the shape of a valley or the layers in a cliff, geologists can read information about climate, time, and the forces that shaped that place.

From this perspective, I define writing as any system that encodes and communicates information in a form that can be interpreted. That might be mathematical notation on a board, hieroglyphics on a wall, or strata in a canyon. All of these are ways of recording something so that it can be read later, by someone else—or even by ourselves in the future.

Who is a Writer?

If writing is any system that encodes information, then a writer is anyone—or anything—that creates those systems and leaves those traces. That certainly includes authors of books and essays, but it also includes mathematicians, scientists, designers, and even natural forces.

Mathematicians are writers when they compose proofs, define new concepts, or model a real-world situation with equations. A proof in a textbook is not just “doing math”; it is a crafted piece of writing meant to be read, interpreted, and built on by others. The same is true for a well-labeled graph, a clear diagram, or a set of carefully written definitions.

In the context of hiking and geology, nature itself can function as a writer. Rivers carve canyons, glaciers leave behind moraines, and tectonic forces fold rock layers. Hikers then become both readers and writers: we read the landforms to understand where we are, and our footpaths, cairns, and trail reports create new marks and records for future hikers.

In this expanded view, writers include people, communities, tools, and environments that participate in shaping and transmitting information. A meme online, a contour map, a topographic profile, or a worn trail can all be forms of writing, and the creators behind them are all, in different ways, writers.

The Object

This website is the object that answers the core questions of the project. Its structure, design, code, and interactive layout demonstrate how writing extends beyond alphabetic text into digital forms. The background images and examples draw specifically from my interests in mathematics, hiking, and geology.

The references to fluvial erosion and landscape features connect writing to geological processes, while the language of functions, relationships, and systems echoes mathematical ways of thinking. Together, these examples show how different disciplines “write” the world in different but related ways.

The project also includes a printed QR code, produced through xerography. The printout acts as a physical extension of the digital text, linking physical space (like a classroom or hallway) to this online space. In this way, the project intentionally uses multiple media to explore what counts as writing and who can be considered a writer.

Reflection

Creating this project helped me connect course ideas about writing to my own experiences as a mathematics major and someone who enjoys hiking and geology. In math, I am used to thinking in terms of symbols, equations, and proofs, but this project made me think of those forms as writing—carefully composed texts meant to be read and interpreted by others.

Hiking and learning about geology also influenced my thinking. Trails, rock layers, and river channels all function like texts that can be read for information about time, force, and change. Seeing these as forms of writing expanded my idea of who or what can be a writer, including natural processes and the people who study and move through those environments.

Technically, this project blends multiple media: HTML/CSS for an interactive digital text, and xerography through a printed QR code that circulates the project in physical space. Conceptually, it blends disciplines, using mathematics and geology as lenses for understanding how writing encodes and communicates information.
